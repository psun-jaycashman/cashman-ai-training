## Lesson 1 — Writing a Report from Scratch

**Lesson runtime estimate:** ~3 min
**Activity at end of lesson:** Exercise — produce a formal monthly progress report from raw notes

### Title card

> **Lesson 1 — From Notes to Report**
> Bullet points in. Polished report out.

### Script

Here's the truth about reports: nobody loves writing the first draft. The hardest part isn't the *thinking*. It's the *typing*. Turning a head full of facts into a clean, organized document.

This is exactly the job AI was built for. You give it your raw notes. It gives you back a structured first draft. Your job changes from writer to **reviewer** -- and that's a much faster job.

There's a structure I want to introduce that's used in almost every formal report. It's called the **executive summary**. That's a short paragraph -- usually three or four sentences -- at the very top that tells a busy reader the bottom line. *Where are we, are we ahead or behind, and is there anything they need to do?* If your report has nothing else, it should have a good executive summary. Most senior people will only ever read that.

Below the executive summary, a strong report has predictable sections: schedule, budget, safety, work completed, upcoming work, and issues. AI knows this structure already. You don't have to spell it out -- but it doesn't hurt to.

Here's a sample prompt: *"Take these notes and write a formal monthly progress report for the USACE contracting officer. Include an executive summary, then sections for schedule, budget, safety, completed work, upcoming work, and open issues. Use professional tone. Keep specific numbers from the notes."*

That last line -- "keep specific numbers from the notes" -- is doing real work. Without it, AI sometimes rounds your numbers, smooths over your details, or quietly drops things. By telling it to preserve specifics, you get a report that's accurate to your source.

And here's the warning that comes with every AI-generated document: watch for **hallucinations**. AI sometimes "improves" a report by inventing specifics that sound plausible -- a CPARS score you never gave it, a date that wasn't in your notes, a percentage that came out of thin air. Read the draft against your source notes line by line. Anything new in the report that wasn't in the notes is suspect.

Down below you'll find raw notes from a real-style USACE wharf rehabilitation project. Paste them into AI. Use the prompt I gave you. Edit the result. Then paste your final report into the exercise.

## Lesson 2 — Rewriting and Editing

**Lesson runtime estimate:** ~2 min 45 sec
**Activity at end of lesson:** Exercise — polish a rough closeout summary

### Title card

> **Lesson 2 — Rewriting and Editing**
> Take a rough draft. Make it shine.

### Script

Sometimes you don't need a brand new document. You need to take something you already wrote -- or that someone handed you -- and *fix it*. Tighten it. Restructure it. Make the tone right for the audience.

This is one of AI's best tricks. You hand it a rambling, casual paragraph. You ask it to make it concise, well-structured, and formal. Thirty seconds later you have a polished version.

The principle behind this is what writers call **editing for the audience**. The same content can be too casual for a board meeting and too stiff for a teammate. AI is great at sliding the dial. You just have to *tell it where the dial should be*. "Formal, suitable for a federal client." "Direct, suitable for a busy peer." "Empathetic, suitable for a crew briefing." The more precisely you describe the audience, the better the rewrite.

Now -- and this is the lesson where this matters most -- I have to bring back **hallucinations**. When AI rewrites a document, it sometimes adds things. A specific date. A named hurricane. A precise CPARS score. A safety statistic. *None of these were in the original.* The AI made them up because they made the rewrite sound more credible.

This is a really good drill. After AI rewrites the document below, do a side-by-side. Anything in the rewrite that wasn't in the original -- a number, a name, a date -- is suspect. If you find an invented detail, congratulations. You just caught a hallucination in the wild.

There's actually a name for the related habit you're building here. It's called **source-grounding**. It means: every claim in your final document should be traceable back to a source you actually have. AI may not understand source-grounding. *You* enforce it.

## Lesson 3 — Creating Templates and Checklists

**Lesson runtime estimate:** ~2 min 15 sec
**Activity at end of lesson:** Exercise — paste in a reusable template you generated

### Title card

> **Lesson 3 — Templates and Checklists**
> Build it once. Use it forever.

### Script

The smartest people I know don't do their work twice. They do it once -- and then they turn it into a template.

If you write a daily report every morning, you should have a daily report template. If you brief your crew on safety topics every Monday, you should have a safety briefing template. If you respond to **RFIs** -- that's a Request for Information, the formal back-and-forth between contractor and engineer or owner -- you should have an RFI response template.

AI makes building these templates almost effortless.

Here's the trick. Describe to AI exactly what the document needs. The sections. The audience. The information that goes in each part. Then ask it to give you back a **template with placeholder fields**. Placeholders are usually written in square brackets -- like `[Project Name]`, `[Date]`, `[Crew Count]` -- so you know exactly where to fill in the project-specific details each time.

There's a real productivity concept behind this called **standard work**. It comes from manufacturing -- specifically, lean methodology -- and it just means: when a task is done frequently, define the best way to do it once, then repeat. Templates are standard work for documents.

Here's the bonus tip. Once you find a *prompt* that produces a great template, **save the prompt itself**. Drop it into a "good prompts" file -- a Word doc, a OneNote page, whatever works. Now you have two reusable assets: the template and the prompt that generated it.

For this exercise, think about a document you write over and over at work. Could be a weekly meeting agenda, a safety briefing, a submittal review, a daily log -- anything. Describe it to AI. Ask for a template with clear placeholder fields. Paste the template into the exercise box. That's a wrap on Module 3.
