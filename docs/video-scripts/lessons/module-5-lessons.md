## Lesson 1 — Creating Images for Presentations

**Lesson runtime estimate:** ~2 min 30 sec
**Activity at end of lesson:** Exercise — paste in your image generation prompt and a description of the result

### Title card

> **Lesson 1 — Creating Images**
> No design skills required.

### Script

You need a cover image for a safety briefing presentation. You're not a graphic designer. You don't have time to dig through stock photo sites. What now?

Now you describe the image you want, and AI makes it.

This is called **generative AI for images**, and it works on the same general idea as the chatbots we've been using -- AI has seen an enormous number of images paired with descriptions, and it learned the patterns. So when you describe an image, it can generate something that matches the description. The most common tools you'll hear about are **DALL-E**, **Midjourney**, and **Stable Diffusion**, but for our work, the image generator inside the Cashman AI Portal is the safe default.

Now, the difference between a great AI-generated image and an amateur one almost always comes down to the **prompt**. Image prompting is its own little skill. Here's the framework I use.

**Subject** -- what's in the image. *"Marine construction workers operating a crane on a barge."*

**Composition** -- how it's framed. *"Wide shot, sunrise lighting, low angle from the water."*

**Style** -- what it looks like. *"Photorealistic, professional corporate photography style, no text overlays."*

**Exclusions** -- what you don't want. *"No watermarks, no cartoon style, no extra hands."*

If you write all four into your prompt, your hit rate goes way up.

Now -- the warning. The same technology that lets us generate useful images means **anyone can create convincing fake photos**. We call those **deepfakes**. A fake image of a "completed" project. A fabricated safety incident photo. A doctored site condition. Used to be these took a graphic designer hours. Now they take ninety seconds.

The defense is simple but important. If a photo supports a financial or legal claim -- an invoice, a damages claim, a delay justification -- treat it the way you'd treat any other piece of evidence. Verify the source. Look for telltale signs of AI generation: oddly smooth textures, inconsistent shadows, six fingers on a hand, text that doesn't quite read right. When in doubt, ask.

For the exercise, generate an image for a marine construction safety briefing cover slide. Use the four-part framework. Paste your prompt and a description of the result.

## Lesson 2 — Presentations with AI

**Lesson runtime estimate:** ~2 min 45 sec
**Activity at end of lesson:** Exercise — paste in a 10-slide outline AI generated from project notes

### Title card

> **Lesson 2 — Presentations with AI**
> Outline first. Detail second.

### Script

Building a slide deck from scratch can eat half a day. AI can give you a structured first draft in a few minutes. But there's a *right* way and a *wrong* way to use it.

The wrong way is to ask AI to "create a 10-slide deck about my project." You'll get something generic, full of buzzwords, that you'll throw away.

The right way is what I call the **two-step pattern**.

**Step one: ask for the outline.** Just the slide titles and a one-sentence purpose for each. Then you read it. You decide if the structure makes sense. You move slides around. You add slides AI missed. You delete the filler.

**Step two: ask for slide content** -- bullet points, talking points, supporting data -- one slide at a time, or in small batches. Now AI has your approved structure to anchor to, and the output is much tighter.

This pattern has a name in writing and design circles: it's called **progressive elaboration**. Start at the highest level. Get alignment. Then add detail. It's how good plans, good documents, and good presentations all get built.

A tool tip while we're here. **Microsoft Copilot in PowerPoint** can do this entire workflow inside the app. You give it a description and it builds the deck. It can also generate slide images on the fly. Just be aware: those slide images are AI-generated, which means -- back to the deepfake warning -- they should never be presented as actual photos of real Cashman work unless you label them as illustrations.

One more concept to know: the **audience**. Who's in the room changes the deck completely. A federal client wants compliance and metrics. An internal team wants honest issues and asks. A potential client wants capability and credibility. *Tell AI who the audience is.* That single line changes the output more than you'd expect.

For the exercise, you'll get notes for a $6 million bulkhead replacement project kickoff. Ask AI for a 10-slide outline. Use the two-step pattern. Paste the outline into the exercise box.

## Lesson 3 — Video, Audio, and Transcription

**Lesson runtime estimate:** ~2 min 45 sec
**Activity at end of lesson:** Exercise — paste in a follow-up email AI generated from meeting notes

### Title card

> **Lesson 3 — Video, Audio, Transcription**
> Meetings document themselves now.

### Script

Last lesson of Module 5. We've covered images and slides. Now -- audio and video.

Quick reality check: how often do you leave a meeting and forget to send the follow-up? Or skip taking notes because someone else is supposedly handling it? AI just removed that whole class of problem.

Microsoft Teams, Zoom, and Otter.ai can all now do **automated transcription** -- that's just turning spoken words into typed text. They can also do **speaker diarization**, which is the technical term for *figuring out who said what*. Combine those, and your meeting produces a complete, searchable transcript. AI then takes the transcript and gives you back a summary, a list of action items with owners, and even a draft follow-up email.

That changes the whole shape of meetings. The team can focus on the conversation instead of frantically taking notes. After the meeting, a single person reviews and corrects the AI output. Done.

There's also a related capability worth knowing about. AI can now **generate** audio and video, not just transcribe it. AI-generated voice is called **voice cloning** -- you can recreate a real person's voice from just a few seconds of sample audio. AI-generated video is called **synthetic video**. The instructor avatar at the start of Module 1, for example, was generated this way.

Which means, of course -- and you knew this was coming -- there's a security issue.

There have been real, documented cases of finance professionals receiving an "urgent" voicemail from someone who sounds *exactly* like their CEO, asking for a wire transfer. The voice is real. The instruction is fake. The whole thing was generated by AI.

The defense is simple, and it's the same defense for almost every AI-era security risk: **verify through a separate channel**. If the request is unusual -- especially financial or legally consequential -- you don't reply to the email. You don't return the voicemail to the same number. You call them back on a phone number you already have. You walk to their office. You confirm in person. Always.

For the exercise, describe a recent or typical meeting in a few bullets. Ask AI to generate the summary, action items, and follow-up email. Paste the email into the box. That wraps Module 5.
