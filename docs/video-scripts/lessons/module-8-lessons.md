## Lesson 1 — Claude Cowork for Research and Analysis

**Lesson runtime estimate:** ~2 min 45 sec
**Activity at end of lesson:** Exercise — paste a complex analysis prompt, response, and notes on what to verify

### Title card

> **Lesson 1 — Claude Cowork**
> The tool for the hard problems.

### Script

We've spent seven modules on the everyday stuff -- email, reports, spreadsheets, search. This module is about the tool you reach for when the question is *bigger*.

That tool is **Claude Cowork**.

Claude Cowork is built for what the AI industry calls **deep reasoning** -- tasks that require sustained, multi-step thinking rather than a quick answer. Evaluating a 100-page contract for risk clauses. Comparing three project delivery methods on a complex bid. Stress-testing a proposal before it goes out the door. Synthesizing research from multiple sources into a coherent recommendation.

The reason Cowork is good at this comes down to two things.

**One: long context.** I introduced the term **context window** earlier in the course -- that's how much text a model can hold at once. Cowork has a very large context window, which means it can ingest a whole contract or a whole research file and reason across all of it without forgetting the beginning by the time it gets to the end.

**Two: extended reasoning.** Some AI models are tuned to think longer before answering. They essentially generate a private "scratchpad" where they work through a problem step by step, then produce a final answer. The technical term for this is **chain-of-thought reasoning**. Cowork uses it for hard problems and it makes a real difference on complex analysis.

Here's the workflow that works well in practice.

**Frame the problem precisely.** State the question, the constraints, and what a good answer looks like.

**Provide the source material.** Drop in the contract, the proposal, the data, the prior emails -- whatever's relevant.

**Ask for structured analysis.** *"Identify the risk clauses. For each one, summarize the risk, rate the severity, and suggest mitigation."*

**Iterate.** Cowork is conversational. If the first answer misses something, point at it. *"You missed the indemnification clause. Reanalyze with that included."*

The same caveats from earlier modules still apply -- maybe more so, because the output looks so polished. **Verify the citations.** **Spot-check the conclusions.** And remember: Cowork is best for research and analysis where the *thinking* is the value. For company-data tasks, the Cashman AI Portal is still the right tool because it handles the data securely.

Your task is below. Pick a complex analysis question relevant to your work. Run it. Note what was helpful and what you'd need to verify before acting on it.

## Lesson 2 — Claude Code for Technical Users

**Lesson runtime estimate:** ~2 min 30 sec
**Activity at end of lesson:** Exercise — paste a description and an automation suggestion

### Title card

> **Lesson 2 — Claude Code**
> Automation for the technically-inclined.

### Script

Quick truth: this lesson isn't going to be relevant to everyone in the company, and that's okay. **Claude Code** is a power-user tool aimed at people who are comfortable with technical work -- scripting, command-line tools, basic programming. If that's not you, no problem -- just understanding *what's possible* helps you spot opportunities and route them to the right person.

So what does Claude Code actually do?

Think of it as an AI pair programmer. You describe what you want -- in plain English -- and it writes the code. Then it can run the code, debug it, and iterate based on the results. It's particularly good at **task automation**: turning a repetitive manual workflow into a script that just runs.

A few examples of what Claude Code is great at:

**Batch file processing.** Renaming hundreds of project files based on a pattern. Reorganizing a folder structure. Converting file formats.

**Data scripts.** Parsing CSV files, cleaning data, generating reports, deduping records.

**Quick utilities.** Building a small calculator. A formatting tool. A data converter.

**Debugging.** Pasting in an error message and asking for an explanation and a fix.

There's a concept here from software engineering that's worth knowing: **automation ROI**. Not every repetitive task is worth automating. The rough rule is: how much time do you spend on it, how often does it happen, and how reliable does the automation need to be? A 30-second task you do once a year? Don't bother. A 30-minute task you do every week? Automate it. The math is straightforward.

There's also a related principle: **the human stays accountable**. Automation runs unattended. If it makes a mistake at scale, the consequences are at scale. That means **logging** -- keeping a record of what the automation did -- and **monitoring** -- checking that record regularly -- are part of the job.

If you're not technical, here's the takeaway: when you find yourself doing the same multi-step task over and over, *flag it*. Talk to IT or to a technical colleague. Many of those tasks can be automated with Claude Code in a single afternoon. You don't need to write the script yourself to get the benefit.

For the exercise, describe a repetitive task from your work. If you're technical, walk through how you'd automate it. If not, ask AI whether it could be automated and how. Paste both into the box.

## Lesson 3 — Building Your AI Workflow

**Lesson runtime estimate:** ~3 min
**Activity at end of lesson:** Survey — Course Completion & Your AI Plan

### Title card

> **Lesson 3 — Your Personal AI Workflow**
> Tool for tool. Risk for risk. Habit for habit.

### Script

This is the last lesson of the course. So let's pull it all together.

You now have an AI **toolkit**, a set of **habits**, and a working knowledge of the **risks**. The goal of this final lesson is to turn that into a personal workflow you can actually use on Monday morning.

Start with this decision tree. Five questions, and you'll always know which tool to reach for.

**One:** *Does the task involve company data?* If yes, use the **Cashman AI Portal**.

**Two:** *Is it Office work -- email, document, spreadsheet, slide deck?* If yes, use **Microsoft Copilot** inside that app.

**Three:** *Does it need deep, sustained reasoning -- a complex analysis or research task?* Use **Claude Cowork**.

**Four:** *Does it involve automation or scripting?* Use **Claude Code**, or flag it to a technical colleague.

**Five:** *Is it general knowledge or learning?* Any tool works -- including **ChatGPT** for public, non-sensitive content.

That's the whole decision framework.

Now -- the security recap, because every module had a piece of this. Let me give you the consolidated checklist. There's a name for what we're building here: a personal **threat model**. That's just security-speak for *the catalog of things that can go wrong, and what you do about each one.*

**Hallucinations** -- AI invents plausible facts. Defense: source-grounding and verification.

**Prompt injection** -- hidden instructions in input data. Defense: review AI output, especially when input came from outside.

**Wrong formulas** -- AI calculations that look right but aren't. Defense: test with known values.

**Deepfakes** -- fake images, video, or voice. Defense: verify through a separate channel for any consequential claim.

**Data leakage** -- company data ending up in a public AI service. Defense: company data goes to the Portal, period.

**Agent over-trust** -- assuming agent output is correct because it cites sources. Defense: click the citations, spot-check the math, keep a human in the loop.

Print that list. Tape it to your monitor. That's your AI security cheat sheet.

The final piece of this course is a short survey down below. It asks how you plan to use AI in your work, and gathers your feedback on the training. Complete it and you'll earn the **Think (AI)mpossible** badge and your Cashman AI Training certificate.

You did the work. You earned this. Welcome to the new toolkit. Let's go build something with it.
