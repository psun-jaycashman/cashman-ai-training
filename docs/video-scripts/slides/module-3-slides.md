### Slide B3 — Define Executive Summary
- **Layout:** Term + definition
- **Headline:** New term: Executive Summary
- **Body:** A short paragraph (3–4 sentences) at the top of a report that gives a busy reader the bottom line.
- **Visual:** Report mockup with the top section highlighted.
- **Timing:** 0:35–1:00

### Slide B4 — Standard Report Sections
- **Layout:** Vertical list
- **Headline:** Most reports have predictable sections:
- **Body:**
  - Executive Summary
  - Schedule
  - Budget
  - Safety
  - Work Completed
  - Upcoming Work
  - Issues
- **Visual:** Stacked section blocks; AI knows this structure.
- **Timing:** 1:00–1:25

### Slide B5 — Sample Prompt
- **Layout:** Code/quote block
- **Headline:** Sample prompt:
- **Body:** *"Take these notes and write a formal monthly progress report for the USACE contracting officer. Include an executive summary, then sections for schedule, budget, safety, completed work, upcoming work, and issues. Keep specific numbers from the notes."*
- **Visual:** Prompt → arrow → finished report.
- **Timing:** 1:25–1:55

### Slide B6 — Why "Keep Specific Numbers"
- **Layout:** Tip card
- **Headline:** That last line matters.
- **Body:** Without it, AI sometimes rounds, smooths, or drops your specifics.
- **Visual:** Two columns — numbers in source notes preserved in the report (highlighted).
- **Timing:** 1:55–2:15

### Slide B7 — Hallucination Drill
- **Layout:** Warning card
- **Headline:** Always check for hallucinations.
- **Body:** Read the draft against your source. Anything new in the report that wasn't in the notes is suspect.
- **Visual:** Side-by-side: notes vs report. Red callout on an invented fact.
- **Timing:** 2:15–2:40

### Slide B8 — Action
