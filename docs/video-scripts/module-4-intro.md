# Module 4 Intro — Spreadsheets and Data

- **Instructor:** Peter
- **Target runtime:** ~80 seconds
- **Audience:** Cashman employees, brand new to AI
- **Goal:** Make Excel feel approachable again, preview the three lessons (one of them on a real-shaped $32M project), and introduce the "always test it" rule.

## Script

Hey, I'm Peter. Welcome to Module 4.

Quick question: when you need a tricky Excel formula, what do you do? If you're like most people, you Google it, click on a forum from 2014, scroll past four ads, and then try to adapt some stranger's example to your spreadsheet.

Those days are over.

In this module, you're going to learn how to just *describe* what you want -- in normal English -- and let AI write the formula for you. "I need column G to give me Total Billed by adding Previously Billed and This Period." That's it. AI hands you the formula.

We're going to cover three things.

First, **AI-powered formulas.** And this one's not a toy example -- you'll work on a downloadable schedule of values for a hypothetical thirty-two-million-dollar marine project, with twenty-one line items, division subtotals using SUMIFS, status flags, retention math, the whole picture.

Second, **data analysis.** AI can suggest *how* to look at a dataset -- what to compare, what chart to build, what story the numbers might be telling. It's like having a junior analyst on call.

Third, **cleaning up messy data.** Equipment logs from three different superintendents, all in different formats? AI can normalize that into a clean table in seconds.

But here's the rule for this whole module: **always test it.** AI formulas usually *look* right, but a wrong cell reference or an inverted condition can quietly break a report -- and on a thirty-two-million-dollar pay app, that's a real-money mistake. Plug in a number where you already know the answer, and make sure the formula gives you that answer.

Trust, but verify. Let's go.
